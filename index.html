Problem Statement

Traditional robotic systems are often limited to fixed environments and lack flexibility when dealing with dynamic object locations. Our project addresses this by developing an autonomous mobile robotic arm capable of detecting an object, navigating toward it, and preparing for pick-and-place interaction through an integrated mechanical, electrical, and software system.

Abstract

This project presents the design and implementation of an autonomous mobile robotic arm system for object detection, navigation, and pick-and-place preparation. The system combines a mobile platform, a robotic arm, ROS2-based control, OpenCV vision tracking, encoder-based navigation feedback, and a structured electrical power architecture. Mechanical components were designed and optimized for fabrication using 3D printing, while load-sensitive parts were strengthened according to stress and torque considerations. On the control side, a simplified URDF model of the platform was built in ROS2, and OpenCV-based tracking was used to detect and follow a ball target. The platform scans its environment, locates the object, navigates toward it, and stops at a defined proximity range.

Background - Literature Review

Autonomous robotics combines sensing, actuation, embedded control, and software coordination to allow machines to perform tasks with minimal human intervention. Mobile robotics commonly relies on encoders, feedback control, and perception systems for navigation, while robotic manipulators require careful torque analysis, actuator selection, and structural design. This project builds on those ideas by combining a mobile base with a robotic arm and integrating them through ROS2, OpenCV, URDF modeling, and real hardware power distribution.

Methods

Engineering Analysis (Calculations and Simulations)

Sensing and Signal Architecture

  • System Definition and Primary Sensing: OpenCV-based vision was used for target detection and tracking.
  • Signal Conditioning and Conversion: Image input was processed to estimate target location relative to the platform.
  • Digital Logic and Data Presentation: ROS2 and RViz2 were used to observe robot behavior and support decision logic.

Actuation and Mechanical Drive

  • Fluid Power and Kinematic Chains: Vacuum gripper concept was studied and suction requirements were estimated.
  • Electrical Actuation and Motor Selection: Servo motors were considered for the arm, and DC motors were analyzed for the platform.
  • Embedded Controller Selection: ROS2-compatible onboard control architecture was used to coordinate movement and tracking.

Control Logic and Programming

  • Firmware Development: A simple platform URDF was created in ROS2 using wheel dimensions matching the real platform.
  • I/O Interfacing and Industrial Control: Encoder behavior was studied to support future closed-loop navigation.
  • Communication and Networking: ROS2 was used as the communication framework between platform modeling, tracking logic, and navigation behavior.

Advanced Modeling and Optimization

  • Mathematical System Modeling: Stress, load, and torque effects were considered in print parameter selection and structural design.
  • Stability and Feedback Control: Target tracking logic was designed so the robot scans, detects, approaches, and stops at a proximity of about 20 cm.
  • Intelligent Control and Final Design: The platform first performs a 360-degree scan, detects the ball, and then navigates toward it autonomously.

Testing

Prototype Construction

  • Initial Design: CAD models and simplified URDF models were developed for testing system behavior in simulation.
  • Final Design: Manufacturing-focused optimization was applied to 3D printed components based on stress and structural requirements.

Final Integration and Demonstration

  • Testing Environment: ROS2 simulation, OpenCV tracking, and real electrical hardware connection tests were used.
  • Success / Fail or Pass Criteria: The system is considered successful if it can detect the object, navigate toward it, and stop at the target range while maintaining stable behavior.
  • Results: The tracking and navigation logic was demonstrated in simulation, and the electrical subsystem underwent initial real-life connection testing.
  • Discussion: The project has progressed from conceptual design into manufacturing, hardware integration, and autonomous behavior testing, with full validation still ongoing.
