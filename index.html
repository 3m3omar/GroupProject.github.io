Problem Statement

Traditional robotic systems are often limited to fixed environments and lack flexibility when dealing with dynamic object locations. This project addresses that limitation by developing an autonomous mobile robotic arm capable of detecting an object, navigating toward it, and preparing for object interaction through an integrated mechanical, electrical, and software system.

Abstract

This project presents the design and implementation of an autonomous mobile robotic arm system for object detection, navigation, and pick-and-place preparation. The system combines a mobile platform, robotic arm, ROS2-based control, OpenCV vision tracking, encoder-based motion feedback, and a structured electrical architecture. Mechanical components were optimized for fabrication using 3D printing, while load-sensitive parts were strengthened based on stress and torque considerations. A simplified URDF platform model was developed in ROS2 to test encoder behavior and tracking logic. The navigation behavior is based on scanning the environment, locating the ball, approaching it, and stopping at a target proximity.

Background - Literature Review

Autonomous robotic systems combine sensing, actuation, feedback control, and software coordination to perform tasks with minimal human intervention. Mobile robotics commonly depends on encoder feedback and perception-based navigation, while robotic manipulators require careful torque analysis, actuator selection, structural design, and stable control. This project combines both domains into a single integrated mechatronics system using ROS2, OpenCV, URDF modeling, and real hardware power distribution.

Methods

Engineering Analysis (Calculations and Simulations)

Sensing and Signal Architecture

  • System Definition and Primary Sensing: OpenCV-based vision is used for object detection and tracking.
  • Signal Conditioning and Conversion: Camera input is processed to estimate target position relative to the platform.
  • Digital Logic and Data Presentation: ROS2 and RViz2 are used to monitor robot behavior and support decision logic.

Actuation and Mechanical Drive

  • Fluid Power and Kinematic Chains: A vacuum gripper concept was studied and suction requirements were estimated from object weight.
  • Electrical Actuation and Motor Selection: Servo motors were considered for the robotic arm and DC motors for the mobile platform.
  • Embedded Controller Selection: A ROS2-compatible onboard control architecture is used to coordinate tracking and movement.

Control Logic and Programming

  • Firmware Development: A simplified URDF model of the platform was created using real wheel dimensions.
  • I/O Interfacing and Industrial Control: Encoder behavior was studied to support future closed-loop motion control.
  • Communication and Networking: ROS2 is used to connect tracking logic, platform motion, and simulation behavior.

Advanced Modeling and Optimization

  • Mathematical System Modeling: Stress, load, and torque effects were used to guide structural optimization and print settings.
  • Stability and Feedback Control: Tracking logic was designed so the platform scans, detects, approaches, and stops near the object.
  • Intelligent Control and Final Design: The robot performs a 360-degree scan, detects the ball, navigates toward it, and stops at about 20 cm.

Testing

Prototype Construction

  • Initial Design: CAD models and simplified URDF models were developed for testing system behavior in simulation.
  • Final Design: Manufacturing-focused optimization was applied to 3D printed components based on stress and structural requirements.

Final Integration and Demonstration

  • Testing Environment: ROS2 simulation, OpenCV tracking, and real electrical hardware connection tests were used.
  • Success / Fail or Pass Criteria: The system is successful if it detects the object, navigates toward it, and stops at the target range while remaining stable.
  • Results: Tracking and navigation logic were demonstrated in simulation, and the electrical subsystem underwent initial real-life connection testing.
  • Discussion: The project has progressed from conceptual design into fabrication, hardware integration, and autonomous behavior testing, with full validation still ongoing.
